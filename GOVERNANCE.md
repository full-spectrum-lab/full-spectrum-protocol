# Governance

Full Spectrum is a protocol exploration. Its governance must match its own principles: no single subject should claim to represent the whole.

## Core governance principle

Protocol changes should be:

- traceable;
- reviewable;
- reversible when possible;
- open to disagreement;
- explicit about risk, abuse, and responsibility.

## Maintainer roles

Suggested roles:

- **Protocol maintainer**: keeps protocol structure coherent.
- **Documentation maintainer**: keeps documents readable and correctly encoded.
- **Engineering maintainer**: keeps schemas, demos, and examples usable.
- **Safety reviewer**: reviews abuse cases and failure modes.
- **Guardian reviewer**: reviews whether a change violates the Compassion Protocol.

One person may hold multiple roles in early stages, but role boundaries should remain visible.

## Change process

For small documentation fixes:

1. Open an issue or pull request.
2. Explain what is being corrected.
3. Merge after maintainer review.

For protocol-level changes:

1. Create an RFC.
2. State the problem.
3. State the proposed change.
4. State affected modules.
5. State abuse risks.
6. State rollback or compatibility plan.
7. Request review.

The first RFC is [RFC 0001: Full Spectrum Protocol](./rfcs/0001-full-spectrum-protocol.md).

## Disagreement

Disagreement is not failure. It is one of the main information sources for protocol evolution.

When disagreement is high:

- record competing positions;
- avoid premature closure;
- request additional review;
- create examples or counterexamples;
- mark unresolved areas clearly.

## Boundary self-knowledge

This project must not claim:

- final authority over ethics;
- authority to speak for all humans, all AI, or all civilizations;
- certainty where only simulation or interpretation exists.

The protocol may assist decision-making, but it must preserve the visibility of uncertainty, dissent, and responsibility.

## Relationship to law and institutions

Full Spectrum does not replace law, human rights frameworks, industry regulation, or organizational governance.

It may serve as:

- a protocol layer;
- an audit layer;
- a simulation layer;
- a review layer;
- an interoperability layer.

Final legal and institutional responsibility remains with the relevant human and organizational authorities.
