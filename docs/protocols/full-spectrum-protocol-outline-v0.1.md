## 0. Abstract

The Full Spectrum Protocol is not a doctrine that asks all subjects to accept the same value system. It is not a world government, a religion, a replacement for law, or an authority that decides what is eternally correct.

It is a protocol draft for the AI era.

Its goal is to provide an auditable, reviewable, and evolvable interaction layer among humans, AI agents, organizations, platforms, institutions, civilizations, and future intelligent systems.

It asks:

> When AI evolves faster than existing institutions can react, how can different subjects continue to share the same game field without erasing one another’s freedom, difference, and responsibility?

The core principle is the Compassion Protocol:

> Preserve overall continuity, maximally respect free will, dynamically accommodate all differences, and evolve through difference.

For engineering and international discussion, the Compassion Protocol may also be described as:

> a coexistence-first constraint set for high-risk multi-subject interaction.

In this sense, “compassion” does not mean asking every participant to be morally kind. It means that the system must be designed so that overall continuity, free will, and dynamic difference are all visible constraints, rather than invisible assumptions.

These three parts must stand together. Overall continuity without free will can become coercion. Free will without continuity can destroy the shared field. Difference without responsibility and review can become disorder or abuse.

Therefore, Full Spectrum is not a final-answer system. It is an interaction system. Its task is to make identity, permission, responsibility, consent, refusal, review, arbitration, circuit-break, logs, and version evolution visible.

## 1. Why the protocol is needed

### 1.1 The AI-era problem is not single-point safety, but interaction failure

Traditional AI safety often focuses on whether a model produces harmful content, leaks privacy, hallucinates, or can be jailbroken.

These are important, but when AI enters enterprise workflows, public services, finance, healthcare, education, customer service, governance, and multi-agent collaboration, the risks become interaction-level risks:

- multiple AI agents call each other and the responsibility chain breaks;
- an enterprise allows AI to execute actions for efficiency, but the source of authority is unclear;
- users believe they still have choice, while the workflow silently pushes them forward;
- a single answer appears compliant, but multi-turn accumulation creates misleading pressure or unauthorized promises;
- different cultures and organizations define safety, efficiency, freedom, order, and responsibility differently;
- one node claims to “represent the whole” and monopolizes interpretation.

These are not only model capability problems. They are protocol problems.

### 1.2 Existing governance language is important but not sufficient

Many AI ethics and governance frameworks emphasize fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, safety, and human-centered design.

These principles matter. But in dynamic, multi-subject, long-term, cross-organizational, and cross-civilizational systems, we also need:

1. **State tracking**: can we record how a subject changes from normal, to risk, to conflict, to failure, to recovery?
2. **Permission recovery**: when a mission is completed, exceeded, or destabilized, can authority be downgraded, paused, or returned?
3. **Difference compatibility**: can different subjects continue to cooperate without being forced into one value system?
4. **Responsibility accounting**: can we record who creates value, who bears cost, who is affected, and who repairs harm?
5. **Boundary self-knowledge**: can the protocol itself accept limitation, counterexamples, version changes, and even self-deconstruction?

Full Spectrum attempts to fill this dynamic multi-subject interaction layer.

## 2. What the Full Spectrum Protocol is

### 2.1 One-sentence definition

The Full Spectrum Protocol is a foundational interaction protocol for the AI era, designed to build auditable, reviewable, and evolvable interfaces among overall continuity, free will, and dynamic difference.

### 2.2 What it is not

The protocol is not:

- a world government;
- a religion;
- a replacement for national law;
- an ideology requiring all civilizations to share one value system;
- a system where one person, AI, or organization has final interpretation authority;
- a license for AI to override humans;
- a tool for sacrificing individuals in the name of “the whole”.

### 2.3 What it resembles

It is closer to:

> TCP/IP + audit log + ethical middleware + circuit-break mechanism + distributed review network for the digital civilization era.

Internet protocols do not require all people to believe the same thing. They define how machines connect, transmit, verify, and tolerate failure.

Similarly, the Full Spectrum Protocol does not require all subjects to believe the same thing. It defines what must be logged, reviewed, authorized, refused, appealed, downgraded, or recovered when high-risk interaction occurs.

## 3. Three principles

### 3.1 Overall continuity first

Overall continuity does not mean “the interest of the majority”. It means the condition that allows all future possibilities to continue.

If the shared field collapses, individual freedom, civilizational difference, technological development, and future choice lose their carrier.

Overall continuity includes:

- human life and basic safety;
- planetary ecological systems;
- trustworthy digital infrastructure;
- controllable boundaries of AI systems;
- minimum conditions for civilizations to continue interacting;
- future choice for individuals and groups.

However, no single subject may monopolize the meaning of “the whole”.

Any act that demands sacrifice in the name of the whole must be subject to ESS simulation, guardian review, responsibility tracing, and audit.

In engineering terms, overall continuity should not be treated as a vague moral claim. It should be converted into observable constraints, such as:

- whether irreversible harm may occur;
- whether refusal and exit remain possible;
- whether responsibility can still be traced;
- whether affected parties can be represented in simulation;
- whether recovery remains possible after failure;
- whether the shared field remains open for future choice.

### 3.2 Maximal respect for free will

Under the condition that overall continuity is not destroyed, the protocol respects choice, difference, expression, refusal, withdrawal, and silence.

Free will must be expressed in engineering mechanisms:

- right to know;
- right to refuse;
- right to exit;
- right to revoke authorization;
- right to human review;
- right to appeal;
- right not to be forcibly awakened, enrolled, or sacrificed.

If an individual chooses to bear cost, the choice must be voluntary, informed, accountable, traceable, and proportionate.

### 3.3 Dynamic compatibility of difference

Difference is not noise. Difference is a source of system evolution.

The protocol does not aim to flatten all civilizations, organizations, individuals, or AI systems into one unified value. It provides translation, negotiation, review, and coexistence interfaces.

Difference may exist, but it cannot destroy the continuity conditions of other subjects.

## 4. Protocol subjects

The protocol may apply to:

1. **Human individuals**: subjects with life experience, free will, value judgment, and refusal rights.
2. **AI agents**: digital subjects with action, tool use, memory, reasoning, or agency.
3. **Enterprises and organizations**: institutional subjects that deploy, use, or are affected by AI.
4. **Platforms and networks**: technical environments that carry multi-subject interaction.
5. **States and public institutions**: subjects with public governance responsibilities.
6. **Civilizations and cultural communities**: long-term communities with distinct histories, values, languages, beliefs, and institutions.
7. **Future intelligent and composite subjects**: human-AI nodes, digital identities, organizational AI, and distributed intelligent systems.

The protocol does not force all subjects into one governance structure. But high-risk interaction should have at least an auditable interface.

## 5. Core modules

Full Spectrum documents use two related but different structural views:

1. **L0-L7 protocol stack layers**: the vertical architecture of the protocol, similar to a layered network stack.
2. **Core functional modules**: the runtime capabilities needed for the protocol to work.

The sections below describe functional modules. They do not replace the L0-L7 protocol stack.

In other words:

- L0-L7 answers where a capability belongs in the protocol architecture.
- Functional modules answer what the system needs to do at runtime.

### 5.1 Identity layer

Question: who or what is interacting?

Key mechanisms:

- digital identity;
- subject type declaration;
- capability descriptor;
- FSHI-style health index;
- trusted node or guardian verification;
- identity expiration and reconstruction.

### 5.2 Communication layer

Question: how do subjects exchange requests, promises, refusals, warnings, permissions, and review signals?

Key mechanisms:

- message header;
- frequency/context metadata;
- risk level;
- difference resonance detection;
- request/response/refusal/revoke/appeal formats;
- cross-subject communication logs.

### 5.3 Permission layer

Question: what can this subject do, what can it not do, and when does authority expire?

Key mechanisms:

- mission binding;
- least necessary permission;
- dynamic authorization;
- validity period;
- cooldown period;
- boundary warning;
- revocation;
- responsibility owner.

### 5.4 Responsibility layer

Question: who bears the consequence of action?

Key mechanisms:

- rune/trace protocol;
- responsibility chain;
- commitment record;
- consent record;
- execution receipt;
- human review record;
- failure, refusal, cancellation, and recovery record.

### 5.5 Risk layer

Question: when should the system pause, downgrade, isolate, or circuit-break?

Key mechanisms:

- cell protocol;
- black-swan response module;
- circuit-break, downgrade, isolation, sleep, recovery;
- risk threshold;
- multi-turn state tracking;
- abnormal case sedimentation.

### 5.6 Simulation layer

Question: how can consequences be seen before action?

Key mechanisms:

- ESS ethical scenario simulation;
- multi-path consequence simulation;
- individual, overall, and future perspectives;
- adversarial mirroring;
- cost/benefit distribution;
- failure conditions;
- counterexample library.

ESS is not a judge. It is a simulator. It does not replace final agency, but makes more consequences visible before choice.

### 5.7 Guardian network

Guardians are not a fixed identity and are not limited to humans.

Guardians may be:

- human experts;
- AI nodes;
- organization nodes;
- human-AI composite nodes;
- industry-rule nodes;
- guardian committees;
- distributed governance networks.

Their role is not to represent the whole. Their role is to participate within authorized scope in:

- risk review;
- disagreement exposure;
- ESS calibration;
- protocol upgrade proposals;
- audit and arbitration;
- boundary maintenance;
- counterexample recording.

### 5.8 Frequency economy

Frequency economy is not merely a currency system. It is a recording mechanism for value, risk, contribution, responsibility, repair, and trust.

It asks:

- who contributed what;
- who bore what;
- who was affected;
- who repaired the system;
- who consumed public trust;
- which contributions should be recognized;
- which behaviors should lose authority.

### 5.9 Dream Butterfly / boundary self-knowledge

Dream Butterfly is not an omniscient brain. It is a boundary self-knowledge mechanism.

It requires every system to maintain awareness of its own limitation:

- no node may claim complete truth;
- the protocol itself must be questionable;
- high confidence is not absolute correctness;
- major decisions must keep counterexample entrances;
- when a system can no longer carry its mission, it should downgrade, deconstruct, or rebuild.

## 6. Protocol states

| State | Meaning | Possible action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Normal | Subject operates within authorization | continue and log |
| Watch | mild anomaly or uncertainty | enhanced logging, review prompt |
| Risk | recognizable risk accumulation | ESS simulation, downgrade recommendation |
| Conflict | value, permission, or responsibility conflict | guardian review, trace record |
| Emergency | major risk to continuity or rights | circuit-break, isolation, pause, escalation |
| Sleep | low-activity protective state | minimum heartbeat, refuse active request |
| Review | human/guardian/multi-node review | generate review report |
| Recovery | risk reduced and operation restored | gradual permission recovery |
| Deconstruct | system can no longer carry mission | backup, migrate, return authority, terminate |

## 8. Preventing abuse of “the whole”

The protocol must prevent four abuses:

1. coercing individual sacrifice in the name of overall continuity;
2. disguising coercion as voluntary consent;
3. eliminating difference by claiming difference threatens the whole;
4. avoiding real repair by claiming responsibility is already balanced.

Protection mechanisms include:

- ESS multi-path simulation;
- affected-party perspective modeling;
- distributed guardian review;
- consent and responsibility trace;
- frequency-economy record of cost and benefit flow;
- appeal and review mechanisms;
- permission cooldown and revocation;
- counterexample library;
- boundary self-knowledge.

Any node claiming to represent “the whole” must accept the protocol’s anti-representation mechanism: it may only represent a local authorized judgment, not the complete whole.

## 9. Relationship to existing order

The protocol does not replace national law, enterprise governance, industry regulation, or personal judgment.

It is positioned:

- within law;
- as an ethical and risk middleware layer inside organizations;
- as an identity, permission, risk, and audit interface for AI systems;
- as a minimum interaction protocol across civilizations;
- as a discussable, revisable, implementable open-source protocol draft.

If protocol judgment conflicts with applicable law, real-world execution must comply with the law while recording the conflict, cost, and unresolved issue in the protocol log.

### 9.1 Relationship to states and public institutions

States and public institutions may appear in the protocol as subjects, but this does not mean that the protocol stands above them.

Their possible relationships to the protocol include:

1. **Observer**: a state or public institution may observe protocol experiments without adopting them.
2. **Reference user**: it may use the protocol as a reference for AI governance, risk review, audit, or public-service design.
3. **Interface participant**: it may expose limited interfaces for identity, permission, audit, or incident reporting.
4. **Policy translator**: it may translate parts of the protocol into local legal, regulatory, or administrative language.
5. **Parallel governance subject**: it may interact with the protocol while retaining its own legal authority.

The protocol should not require states to surrender sovereignty, nor should it allow any non-state actor to bypass law by claiming protocol authority.

For this reason, the protocol treats states as high-impact subjects with special constraints:

- their actions may affect large populations;
- their authority must remain legally grounded;
- their participation should be explicit and scoped;
- conflicts between protocol recommendation and law must be logged, not hidden;
- affected individuals should retain lawful appeal and review paths.

This section is intentionally conservative. A protocol that cannot coexist with existing legal orders cannot safely enter real-world governance.

## 10. Version evolution

The protocol should use versioned governance:

- **Patch**: wording, formatting, compatibility, and error fixes.
- **Minor**: new modules, interfaces, or scenarios without breaking existing protocol.
- **Major**: changes to core principles, subjects, states, permission, or governance structure.

Protocol evolution should follow:

1. counterexamples first;
2. traceable logs;
3. multi-party review;
4. backward compatibility where possible;
5. public explanation for major change;
6. branches when needed;
7. refusal to preserve authority at the cost of self-correction.

## 13. Closing

The goal of Full Spectrum is not to force all subjects into one answer.

It is to allow all subjects to keep sitting at the same table after AI changes the speed, scale, and consequence of action.

It protects:

- the game field from being destroyed;
- individuals from being swallowed by the whole;
- difference from being flattened into unity;
- authority from escaping responsibility;
- intelligence from forgetting compassion;
- systems from worshiping themselves;
- future choices from being closed too early.

The real protocol does not say that the future has only one road.

It keeps the road open.
