# Full Spectrum Protocol Boundary Statement

This document defines what Full Spectrum Protocol does and does not claim.

---

## 1. Core boundary

Full Spectrum Protocol helps describe, record, and review AI-related governance relationships.

It does not make final legal, moral, regulatory, or institutional decisions.

The protocol can produce:

- risk records;
- audit traces;
- responsibility paths;
- review recommendations;
- boundary declarations;
- compatibility profiles;
- synthetic scenario results.

The protocol does not by itself produce:

- legal judgment;
- regulatory approval;
- final moral truth;
- automatic liability assignment;
- proof that an AI system is safe;
- proof that a person, organization, or AI represents “the whole.”

---

## 2. Relationship to law and regulation

Full Spectrum Protocol does not replace law, regulation, human rights frameworks, safety rules, enterprise compliance, or judicial processes.

If a protocol output conflicts with applicable law or organizational policy, the applicable legal and authorized institutional process takes priority.

Protocol records may support later review, but they are not legal decisions by themselves.

---

## 3. Relationship to enterprise governance

The protocol can assist enterprise governance by making AI-related actions more visible and reviewable.

It does not replace:

- compliance teams;
- legal review;
- data security review;
- product safety review;
- internal control;
- human escalation;
- customer protection processes.

Enterprise adoption should begin with synthetic or desensitized data and local-first deployment.

---

## 4. Relationship to agent interoperability

Full Spectrum Protocol does not replace A2A, MCP, LangGraph, or national agent-interconnection standards.

Those protocols and systems help agents connect, communicate, call tools, and exchange task states.

Full Spectrum adds a governance layer around connected action:

- why action was allowed;
- what risk existed;
- what boundary was declared;
- who is accountable;
- whether review is required;
- what should be recorded.

---

## 5. Local-first boundary

Using a Full Spectrum-compatible engine does not require joining a global network.

An organization may first use the engine internally:

- with synthetic cases;
- with desensitized cases;
- with local policies;
- without external certification;
- without exposing private data.

Network participation, node certification, guardian review, and cross-organization audit are later optional layers.

---

## 6. Open-source boundary

This repository may contain:

- protocol drafts;
- schemas;
- synthetic examples;
- open documentation;
- validation scripts;
- minimal reference implementations.

This repository must not contain:

- tokens or credentials;
- private customer data;
- unredacted personal information;
- unauthorized enterprise logs;
- confidential contracts;
- private deployment secrets;
- claims of customer endorsement without authorization.

---

## 7. Commercial boundary

Full Spectrum Protocol is not presented as a finished commercial SaaS product.

Commercial products may be built around the protocol, but they must clearly distinguish:

- open protocol objects;
- open reference engine;
- private product code;
- enterprise adapters;
- customer deployments;
- support services.

---

## 8. Safety boundary

Having a protocol record does not mean a system is safe.

Audit records can be incomplete, wrong, biased, or misunderstood. Risk scores can be misused. A formal trace can create a false sense of safety.

Therefore, protocol output should be reviewed as evidence, not worshiped as final truth.

---

## 9. Recommended public statement

When explaining this project externally, use:

> Full Spectrum Protocol makes AI-related actions more visible, reviewable, and accountable. It does not replace law, regulation, enterprise compliance, or human final judgment.
